George Henry Daniels

From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

George Henry Daniels

Daniels in 1901

Born December 1, 1842
Hampshire, Illinois, US
Died July 1, 1908 (aged 65)
Occupation Publicist
Known for Publicizing New York Central

George Henry Daniels (December 1, 1842 â€“ July 1, 1908) was an American publicist mostly known for his association with the New York Central railroad lines. He published time tables and magazines for the railroad. His Four Track News was a well-known travel magazine that had articles on travel, biography, geography, and literature. He conceived and named the Empire State Express, onetime flagship train for the New York Central & Hudson River Railroad. He also conceived the 20th Century Limited luxury train. Celebrities and famous people rode this train. And it became the setting for an eponymous-named Broadway play and Hollywood movie. He is the one that conceived the red cap railroad porter service and the red carpet service, a new term in the American culture known as the "red carpet treatment".

Early life

Daniels was born on December 1, 1842.[1] He went to the public schools of Hampshire, Illinois and while growing up spent most of his time on a local farm. He got his first job in 1857 when he was 15 years old as an assistant rodman in the engineer corps on the construction of the Northern Missouri Railroad.[2] He was promoted to various duties of higher responsibility in practical railroad work through the years.[1][3]

Mid life

Daniels at the age of thirty in 1872 was laid off from the Northern Missouri Railroad due to bad economic times when the railroad fell into financial difficulty. He got a job then at the Chicago & Pacific Railroad as a freight and passenger manager working out of Chicago.[2] Daniels meanwhile had contributed news of local interest to a newspaper publisher in Chicago between 1857 and 1872. He was writing while employed with the Chicago & Pacific railroad and promoted the company. He wrote up in 1873 a factual archival record of the railroad company activity and worked eight years at the railroad before returning to the Northern Missouri Railroad, which had been taken over by the Wabash, St. Louis and Pacific railroad.[4] He became a general ticket agent for the Wabash system from 1880 to 1882.[1]

Daniels later then for the first ten months of 1882 held a position as the commissioner of the Iowa Trunk-Line Association.[1] In November 1882 he was elected commissioner of the Colorado Traffic Association.[3] He was also manager of the Utah Traffic League when Utah was still a territory. By 1886 he had become the official director of the Central Passenger Board. Later it was named the Central Traffic Association and he was elected vice-chairman of the organization.[1] In time he was promoted to the main director of the Chicago East-bound Passenger Board and that group was of interest to the New York Central railroad lines. The railroad observed Daniels' work and offered him an assignment as manager of the General Passenger Agency headquartered at Grand Central Station in New York City and he accepted the position.[3] He was general passenger agent from 1889 to 1907.[5]

Daniels commanded both the railroads of the New York Central lines and the broadcasting of railroad news stories being put in charge of their advertising bureau.[1] He was not only their general passenger agent but also its prophet.[6] He delivered prunes and apples transporting them by shipment to the east coast of the United States by the Southern Pacific railroad. He had them provided in all railroad dining cars and promoted their excellence in the newspapers.[4] He also had mail that came from Australia and bound for London promoted as a technological achievement. He published in newspapers how mail from Australia that had traveled by steamship to the United States was carried by railroads to the eastern region of the United States and from there sent by boat to its end destination in London and it only took 32 days. With his publicity he showed the world the technology of steam trains in the United States and the products of American resourcefulness and inventiveness.[4]

Four Tracks News

Daniels made railroad timetables that were published for the New York Central and his name was usually on them. He published a travel magazine from Grand Central Station that was called the Four-Track News.[7] The magazine had articles on travel, biography, and literature. New York Central's label given to it in the early 1900s was the "Four Track System". Daniels coined this term highlighting the company's history as being the world's first railroad to have four parallel tracks.[1][8] The magazine was published in the Transit Building at 7 East 42nd Street after 1903.[9]The yearly subscription in 1905 was one dollar (equivalent to $30 in 2021). The magazine advertised in other publications and said that in every one of its issues there were more than twenty articles and 100 illustrations and produced about 1,500 pictures in a year. It claimed that it had quality articles on history and geography that would be a good supplement to school lessons.[10] The magazine continued to be published in some form or adaptation until 2003 under Travel and Travel Holiday.[4][8]

Daniels in 1890 contrived the title "Empire State Express", which was New York Central's newest train at the time that ultimately became the fastest regularly scheduled passenger train in America.[8] He was assigned to promote the train, which made a journey from New York City to the town of Buffalo in under six hours. Daniels suggested the idea of making an exceptionally fast locomotive to pull the five car train, which was done under the direction of locomotive designer William Buchanan.[11] That train engine had the number 999 and ran a speed test of 112.5 miles per hour (181.1 km/h).[12][11] The speed event was specifically designed to get people's attention on the railroad's capability to get them to The 1893 Chicago World's Fair, where the locomotive was exhibited to the public. Daniels even influenced the United States Postmaster General to put out a commemorative stamp featuring locomotive 999 and the Empire State Express.[5][8] Being issued in 1901, it was one of the first stamps available in the 20th century.[4]

20th Century Limited, Daniels's concept of a luxury train with an accelerated speed

A noteworthy achievement credited to Daniels was his conception in 1902 of a luxury train traveling in record time between New York and Chicago.[13] He christened it the 20th Century Limited and fought for launching the development of the state-of-the-art New York Central train.[14][5] The Limited was inaugurated between New York and Chicago on June 15 in 1902 as the train ahead of its time by a hundred years.[5] The 20th Century Limited concept of a faster than normal train that was also a luxury train was an offshoot of the Empire State Express train that Daniels played a role in developing through his creative efforts.[15]

Daniels promoted the New York Central flagship train as the greatest train in the world. It reflected the hope that it could be the originator and pioneer of a new era in high-speed rail transportation. He publicized famous passengers that rode it on a regular bases including the Vanderbilts, Whitneys, and Mellons.[16] It became synonymous with lavish railway excursions.[17][18] Daniels coined the name 20th Century Limited and it symbolized speed and style.[14] Some took Daniels's inspired train from New York City to Albany back and forth routinely for a scenic excursion, forming a travel club and calling themselves Twentieth Century Limited Associates.[16]

Daniels initiated a 20-hour New York-Chicago schedule which many times attracted a larger than normal amount of passengers and required adding additional passenger cars to serve these additional riders.[19] He conceived the idea of publishing free travel brochures about the New York Central lines of where they went, one titled Health and Pleasure on America's Greatest Railroad.[20] He introduced teams of red-cap railroad porter service assistants in 1896 that came from the New York Central lines.[18][20] Daniels also introduced a red carpet service that the 20th Century Limited railroad rolled out along the platform for the boarding passengers in New York City and Chicago.[19][21] This resulted in a new term for the American culture known as the "red carpet treatment".[16][22] He was called the "Father of the Century" since he brought to fruition through his efforts of publicity ingenuity and leadership of the advertisement for the New York Central lines, especially the 20th Century Limited and its elegant luxury service.[5][23]

Clubs and organizations

Daniels was a member of the Lotos Club; a member and once president of the Union Club of the City of New York composed of business and professional men; he aided in the organization of the American Public Transportation Association and was one of its directors; and he was prominent in The Advertising Club of New York.[8]

Later life and death

Daniels in 1905 left his position as New York Central's General Passenger Agent.[24] He then took a position as director of their new advertising department.[24] He occupied that position until 1907 and then retired permanently.[24] He remained with the company in an advisory capacity.[24] Daniels lived his final year of life split between Lake Placid and Buffalo. He died in Lake Placid at the age of 66 on July 1, 1908.[3][4]
